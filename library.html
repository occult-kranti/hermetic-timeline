The library

Books, journal articles, and reputable web resources on Hermeticism, magic, alchemy, and the occult — each tagged by source type so you always know how solid the ground is beneath a claim.

How to read the tags Scholarly means an academic press or peer-reviewed work — the most reliable tier. Journal marks a peer-reviewed article or academic journal. Web marks a reputable but non-peer-reviewed site — useful for primary texts and orientation, best cross-checked against the scholarly sources. Everything listed is chosen for reliability; "bloodline / NWO" content is deliberately excluded except as the object of study in the timeline.

Peer-reviewed journals & articles

  • Aries: Journal for the Study of Western Esotericism. Brill — the principal peer-reviewed journal of the field.Journal
  • Correspondences: Journal for the Study of Esotericism. Open-access, peer-reviewed.Journal
  • Wouter J. Hanegraaff, “Beyond the Yates Paradigm.” Aries 1.1, 2001 — the standard critique of the Yates thesis.Journal
  • Teresa Burns, on the John Dee "007" legend. Journal of the Western Mystery Tradition, 2010 — debunks the spy-signature myth.Journal

Reputable web resources & primary-text archives

  • Twilit Grotto: Esoteric Archives (J. H. Peterson) — meticulously edited Dee diaries, Enochian material, and grimoires.Web
  • The Hermetic Library — large, long-running archive of Hermetic, Thelemic, and ceremonial-magic texts.Web
  • Sacred-Texts: Esoteric & Occult — G. R. S. Mead's Hermetica and other public-domain editions.Web
  • ESSWE — the European Society for the Study of Western Esotericism; conferences and resources.Web
  • Internet Archive — scanned early editions of Agrippa, the Rosicrucian manifestos, Lévi, and more.Web

A note on method. The reliability tag on each entry is the point: scholarly and peer-reviewed work carries the most weight, web archives are valuable for primary texts but should be cross-checked, and conspiracy material is studied in the timeline rather than cited as fact.
