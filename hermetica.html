The practice of the Hermetica

The meditation techniques, rites, and hymns actually preserved in the ancient Hermetic texts — the Corpus Hermeticum, the Asclepius, and the Coptic Hermetica from Nag Hammadi. Their single aim is gnosis: rebirth, the soul's ascent, and union with the divine Mind. This is the real thing the modern "manifestation" tradition borrows its vocabulary from — and it is older, stranger, and far more demanding.

How to read these "steps" The Hermetica are dialogues, not instruction manuals. Where a text gives an explicit instruction — for example, to sing a hymn outdoors facing the dawn — it's marked as such. Everywhere else, the steps below are a faithful reconstruction of the practice's shape from the narrative, and translations differ on detail. These are genuine ancient practices (Roman Egypt, 2nd–3rd century), aimed at knowing and uniting with God, not at producing outcomes or altering events. Read them as the architecture of a contemplative path, with sources to go to the originals.
ICorpus Hermeticum XIII

The Way of Rebirth (palingenesis)

The central transformation rite: to be "born again" in Mind (Nous) — the old, material self dissolved and a new self reconstituted out of the divine Powers. Hermes delivers it to his son Tat on a mountain.

The practice
  1. Prepare and resolve. Tat first steadies his purpose "against the deceit of the cosmos." The work begins with a deliberate turning away from the material self and a readiness to be remade.
  2. Name the twelve tormentors. Hermes identifies the irrational "torments of matter" — vices bound to the twelve zodiac signs (ignorance, grief, intemperance, lust, injustice, greed, deceit, envy, treachery, anger, recklessness, malice). They are the scaffolding of the old self.
  3. Let the ten Powers drive them out. As the seeker yields, the divine Powers — knowledge of God, joy, self-control, perseverance, justice, generosity, truth, and the powers of the Good, Life, and Light — enter and expel the tormentors. The old self "dies"; a new one is assembled from the Powers.
  4. Know the rebirth. Tat finds he now perceives himself not in a body but in Mind — remade as a child of God. The rebirth is an actual shift of identity and perception, not a metaphor.
  5. Sing the Secret Hymn. Hermes imparts the hidden "hymn of silence" that the divine Poimandres sang on entering the higher spheres — to be sung outdoors, bowing facing south at sunset and east at sunrise. It "cannot be taught; it is a secret kept in silence."
  6. Keep silence. The seeker vows to guard the mystery — the "promise to be silent" of the text's title.
Read: CH XIII (Copenhaver, Hermetica; Mead, free online). Commentary: W. C. Grese, CH XIII and Early Christian Literature (Brill, 1979).
IICorpus Hermeticum I (Poimandres)

The Ascent of the Soul

The soul's return to its source, climbing back through the seven planetary spheres and surrendering at each the quality it took on during its descent into matter.

The practice
  1. Receive in stillness. Hermes's revelation comes in a visionary, receptive state — the posture is contemplative reception, not petition.
  2. Know yourself as light. Recognise that the true self comes from the divine Light and Mind, not the body: the one who knows himself advances toward God.
  3. Purify in life. Loosen, while living, the vices tied to the spheres — the same material accretions the soul must later shed.
  4. Ascend through the seven. At the body's dissolution the soul rises sphere by sphere, giving up at each a quality — the energy of increase and decrease, the machination of evils, the deceit of desire, domineering arrogance, unholy daring, the craving for wealth, ensnaring falsehood.
  5. Enter the Ogdoad. Stripped bare, the soul reaches the eighth sphere, joins the powers in hymning the Father, and presses on toward union — becoming, at the last, divine.
Read: CH I (Copenhaver; Mead, free online). Ends with the Poimandres hymn ("Holy is God…").
IIINag Hammadi Codex VI,6

The Ascent to the Eighth & Ninth

The one surviving Hermetic initiation liturgy — a master leads a pupil past the seven spheres into the Ogdoad (Eighth) and Ennead (Ninth), to ecstatic vision and divinisation.

The rite
  1. Prerequisite. The pupil must already have ascended through the earlier stages and undergone rebirth; this is a culmination, not a beginning.
  2. Communal prayer. Master and pupil pray together to the Father with all mind, heart, and soul, asking for the gift of the Eighth and for the power to utter the discourse.
  3. Invocation with intoned vowels. A long apophatic hymn to the One, threaded with sung vowel-sequences — meditational vocalisations built on the sacred name IAO, used like a mantra.
  4. The embrace. Master and pupil embrace with love as "the power, light" begins to arrive.
  5. Vision of the Ogdoad. The master beholds unspeakable depths — Mind seeing Mind — while the souls and angels of the Eighth praise in silence.
  6. The silent hymn & the Ninth. The pupil sings inwardly, in silence, and ascends to behold the Ninth and the One who holds all power.
  7. Seal it. Fall silent, head bowed; make praise lifelong; record the teaching under an oath of purity.
Read free: gnosis.org (Meyer & Barnstone, by permission). Editions: Robinson, The Nag Hammadi Library; Litwa, Hermetica II (Cambridge, 2018).
IVCorpus Hermeticum XI

The Expansion of the Mind

A startling contemplative exercise: since "like is known by like," you can only apprehend God by making your mind God-like — expanding it until it is commensurate with everything that is.

The exercise
  1. Set the premise. Like is known by like; to grasp the boundless, the mind must itself become boundless.
  2. Leap free of the body. In contemplation, release identification with the physical and with time.
  3. Grow without measure. Imagine yourself expanded beyond all limit — higher than every height, lower than every depth, everywhere at once: on land, at sea, in heaven.
  4. Hold all opposites and states. Conceive yourself as containing heat and cold, dry and wet; as unborn, in the womb, young, old, dead, and beyond death — all together.
  5. Gather all times and places. Draw every place, time, quality, and magnitude into a single act of thought.
  6. Apprehend God. When the mind has become as vast as the All, it can know its source. (The inverse is the warning: shrink the soul into the body and its fears, and you cut yourself off entirely.)
Read: CH XI (Copenhaver; Mead, free online). Hanegraaff reads CH XI and XIII as a pair.
VCH X, XIII · NHC VI,6

The Discipline of Silence

Not an empty pause but the summit of the practice: silence (sigē) is the only fit vessel for what cannot be spoken, and the highest form of worship.

The discipline
  1. Let praise end in silence. The hymns themselves culminate in stillness — CH XIII's "hymn of silence," the wordless song of the Eighth and Ninth.
  2. Honour the hidden in stillness. Across the texts, the deepest mysteries are "honoured in silence"; the seeker bows the head and ceases to speak.
  3. Make it continual. The Eighth-and-Ninth ends by enjoining inner praise until the day of leaving the body — silence as a lifelong orientation, not a single act.
Read: CH X & XIII; the Discourse on the Eighth and Ninth.

Corpus Hermeticum I.31

The Poimandres Hymn

Hermes's closing praise to the Father of all — a hymn of the awakened soul, the model prayer that crowns the vision of the Poimandres. Read it in Copenhaver or Mead.

Corpus Hermeticum XIII

The Secret Hymn of Silence

The hidden hymn of rebirth, said to be sung outdoors facing east at sunrise and south at sunset, and which "cannot be taught" — guarded in silence. Read it in Copenhaver or Mead.

Asclepius 41 · NHC VI,7 · PGM III

The Prayer of Thanksgiving

A communal thanksgiving sealed with an embrace and, in the Latin text, a pure meal without animal flesh. One of the very few Hermetic texts surviving in Latin, Greek, and Coptic alike.

One cultic outlier worth naming The Asclepius also preserves the famous "god-making" passage — the old Egyptian temple practice of consecrating statues believed to draw a divine presence into them. It belongs to public temple cult, not personal meditation, and is included in the scholarship as evidence of Hermetism's ritual side rather than as anything to perform. It's noted here only so the picture is complete.

A note on method. These are genuine ancient contemplative practices, reconstructed from dialogue-form texts; where a step is given explicitly in the source it is marked, and translations vary elsewhere. Their aim throughout is rebirth and union with the divine Mind — transformation of the practitioner, not the alteration of past or external events. The modern "revision" and "reality-shifting" practices that borrow this vocabulary are a separate, twentieth-century development, kept distinct across this site.
