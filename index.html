A Chronology of Hermetic Philosophy, Magic & the Occult

As above,so below.

From Babylon and Egypt to the academy · transmission, translation & the line between record and rumour

The maxim above, paraphrased from the Emerald Tablet, became the signature of a tradition that crossed languages and empires — from the star-priests of Babylon and the scribes of Egypt, through Roman Alexandria and the Arabic translators of Harran, to Ficino's Florence, John Dee's angelic tables, and the magical orders of the modern age. This timeline follows that documented thread, and marks where popular history shades into conspiracy.

Starter practices from the tradition

Foundational rituals a beginner historically learns first — described accurately, framed honestly
On “100% effective” These are the genuine entry-level rites of Western ceremonial magic, drawn from published sources. What they reliably do is documented and real: they focus attention, steady the nervous system, induce a calm or mildly altered state, and give shape to an intention — the same mechanisms studied in research on meditation, ritual, and expectancy. Their supernatural efficacy is not something evidence can establish, so nothing here is promised to work as magic. Read this as a practitioner's starter set and a piece of living history, not as a guaranteed cause-and-effect. All five are gentle and safe; none is a substitute for medical or mental-health care.
00Universal · “step zero”

Grounding & centering

Before any rite, practitioners settle the body and breath. Sit upright, relax each part of the body in turn, and breathe slowly — a common pattern is a four-count in, four-count hold, four-count out — until attention rests in the present.

What it actually doesSlow, paced breathing engages the parasympathetic “rest” response, lowering arousal and sharpening focus. It is the contemplative baseline every later practice is built on.
Standard across contemplative traditions; cf. relaxation/meditation research.
01Golden Dawn · 1888

The Qabalistic Cross

A short centering rite, structurally the Western cousin of crossing oneself, using Hebrew words mapped to the Tree of Life:

  1. Touch the forehead — Ateh (“unto Thee”).
  2. Touch the chest — Malkuth (“the Kingdom”).
  3. Touch the right shoulder — ve-Geburah (“and the Power”).
  4. Touch the left shoulder — ve-Gedulah (“and the Glory”).
  5. Clasp the hands at the chest — le-Olahm, Amen (“to the ages, Amen”).
What it actually doesPairs breath, gesture, and spoken word to mark a clear “beginning,” drawing attention inward and establishing a felt center.
Israel Regardie, The Golden Dawn; Crowley, Liber O.
02Golden Dawn · 1888

Lesser Banishing Ritual of the Pentagram

The cornerstone of the whole system, and the first full ritual a member learned. After the Qabalistic Cross:

  1. Facing east, trace a pentagram in the air and intone YHVH.
  2. Turn south, trace the pentagram, intone Adonai.
  3. Turn west, trace it, intone Eheieh.
  4. Turn north, trace it, intone AGLA; return to east to close the circle.
  5. Arms outstretched: “Before me Raphael, behind me Gabriel, on my right Michael, on my left Auriel” — then repeat the Qabalistic Cross.
What it actually doesPractitioners and sceptics alike describe it as a “moving meditation”: the gestures and vibrated names work primarily on the mind, clearing distraction and bounding a space for focused attention.
Regardie, The Golden Dawn; Kraig, Modern Magick.
03Regardie · 1938

The Middle Pillar

A visualization exercise. Imagine a sphere of light at the crown, then draw it down the body's central line — throat, heart, pelvis, feet — pausing at each to breathe and, in the traditional form, intone a divine name, before circulating the light around the body.

What it actually doesA guided body-scan and breath meditation in esoteric dress; reliably produces relaxation, warmth, and concentrated awareness.
Israel Regardie, The Middle Pillar (1938).
04Folk / contemporary

Candle & intention rite

The simplest sympathetic practice. Phrase a single, concrete intention in the present tense; light a candle; hold the intention clearly in mind while watching the flame; let the candle burn down safely (never unattended).

What it actually doesExternalises and sharpens an intention — close to what psychologists call an “implementation intention.” The ritual frame can lower anxiety and reinforce commitment, independent of any supernatural claim.
Widespread in contemporary practice; cf. research on ritual & goal-setting.
05Dee & Kelley · 1580s

Scrying (contemplative gazing)

The practice behind Dee and Kelley's sessions. In low light, gaze softly into a reflective surface — a dark bowl of water, obsidian, or glass — without straining, letting the eyes relax and impressions arise.

What it actually doesSoft, unfocused gazing relaxes visual fixation and can elicit drifting hypnagogic imagery — the documented mechanism behind “visions,” and a calming attentional exercise.
Harkness, John Dee's Conversations with Angels (1999); perception research.

How to read the tags Scholarly means an academic press or peer-reviewed work — the most reliable tier. Journal marks a peer-reviewed article or academic journal. Web marks a reputable but non-peer-reviewed site — useful for primary texts and orientation, best cross-checked against the scholarly sources. Everything listed has been chosen for reliability; popular “bloodline / NWO” content is deliberately excluded except as the object of study in the timeline.

A note on method. This is a curated overview, not an exhaustive register, and dates for ancient and medieval texts are scholarly estimates rather than fixed points. The labels exist to keep a real distinction visible: the Hermetic tradition is a genuine, well-studied strand of intellectual history, while the modern “Illuminati bloodline” and “New World Order” narratives that borrow its imagery have no evidentiary basis and descend from a documented chain of fabrication — not from the sources catalogued here. Where history is uncertain, it is marked disputed; where a specific claim has been disproven, debunked.
