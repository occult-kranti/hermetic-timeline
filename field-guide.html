A field guide to practice Reported folklore

Contemporary practitioners organise their craft by three things: the material they use, the purpose they intend, and how long they expect to wait for a result. This page maps that — drawn from what witchcraft and pagan communities actually say online and in conversation. It is an ethnographic snapshot of belief and practice, not a claim that any of it produces supernatural effects.

Read this as folklore, not physics Everything below is what practitioner communities report — colours, stones, and herbs paired with intentions, and the timeframes people say to expect. Notably, practitioners themselves usually locate the power in focus and intention: "the power comes from the symbolism and purpose behind the ritual, rather than the object." That maps cleanly onto known psychology — attention, expectancy, ritual structure — which is why a practice can feel effective without any supernatural mechanism being involved. Correspondences also vary by tradition and source; treat them as a shared vocabulary, not fixed law.
Safety first — especially with herbs Many plants are not safe to ingest, apply to skin, or burn in an enclosed space — rue, wormwood, tansy, and pennyroyal are toxic, and mugwort is contraindicated in pregnancy. Use herbs symbolically (sachets, charms, altar pieces), research toxicity before any other use, keep flames attended, and treat nothing here as medical, herbal, or psychological advice.

Timing & duration

When practitioners act, and how long they say to wait
Reported timeframes

How long, supposedly

Simple candle or jar workings are commonly said to show results in days to about a week; layered, multi-step spells in weeks to months. A novena — a Catholic-derived form — runs a fixed nine days. These are expectations within the practice, not measured effects.

Lunar timing

The moon cycle

Many work the waxing moon (new → full) to "draw in or increase," and the waning moon to banish or release, charging tools at the full moon. A full cycle is about 28 days — itself a common reported window.

Planetary days

Days of the week

Monday — intuition · Tuesday — courage · Wednesday — communication · Thursday — growth & abundance · Friday — love · Saturday — protection & banishing · Sunday — success. A scheduling lore borrowed from classical planetary correspondences.

A community caution

"That's not how it works"

Even within the practice, a frequent warning: keeping a candle burning longer to force a faster result "isn't how it works." Reported timeframes are framed as patience and "divine timing," not a dial you can turn — a useful tell that this is a belief system, not a mechanism.

Where this comes from These correspondences are drawn from contemporary practitioner communities — witchcraft and pagan blogs, r/witchcraft and r/occult, and "WitchTok" — alongside classic reference works such as Scott Cunningham's Encyclopedia of Magical Herbs. They are treated here as folkloric and ethnographic data: a record of what people do and believe, gathered for understanding, not endorsed as cause and effect.
